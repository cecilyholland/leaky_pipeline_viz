The Leaky Pipeline

A data story about women in tech: where they enter, where they leave, and why we can no longer measure it

Act 1 The Education Pipeline

The pipeline begins in education. Despite decades of outreach, women remain underrepresented in computing degree programs. Progress has been glacial.

Data Source: IPEDS (Integrated Postsecondary Education Data System) — The U.S. Department of Education's comprehensive database of degree completions at all accredited colleges and universities. Years: 2010, 2013, 2016, 2019, 2022, 2024.

Act 2 The Age Cliff

Women who do enter tech often don't stay. Bureau of Labor Statistics data reveals a sharp drop in women's representation around age 35 — the "cliff" where careers derail.

Data Source: BLS Current Population Survey (CPS) — Monthly household survey of ~60,000 households measuring employment by occupation, age, and sex. Women counts by age are estimated (total in bracket x % women in occupation). Years: 2015-2024.

Act 3 The Intersectional View

The cliff doesn't affect all women equally. NSF survey data reveals how race and age intersect to create different experiences in computing careers.

Data Source: NSCG (National Survey of College Graduates) — NSF's biennial survey of ~130,000 college graduates measuring employment in STEM fields. Individual-level microdata with survey weights. Years: 2019 (pre-COVID) and 2023 (post-COVID).

Act 4 The Data Cliff

Just as we began to measure progress, the data is disappearing. Major tech companies have stopped publishing diversity reports entirely.

Data Source: Big Tech Annual Diversity Reports — Self-reported workforce composition data from Google, Apple, Microsoft, Meta, and Amazon. Compiled from public PDF reports published 2014-2024. Note: This data is not independently audited.
