The Leaky Pipeline

A data story about women in tech: where they enter, where they leave, and why we can no longer measure it

Act 1 The Education Pipeline

The pipeline begins in education. Despite decades of outreach programs, scholarships, and initiatives to encourage women in STEM, women remain severely underrepresented in computing degree programs. The progress over 14 years has been glacial — less than half a percentage point per year.

Data Source: IPEDS (Integrated Postsecondary Education Data System)
  • What it is: The U.S. Department of Education's comprehensive database tracking degree completions at all Title IV-eligible postsecondary institutions (over 6,000 colleges and universities)
  • What we used: Completions by CIP code for computing fields (CIP 11.xx) and computer/electrical engineering (CIP 14.09, 14.10)
  • Years covered: 2010, 2013, 2016, 2019, 2022, 2024 (sampled every 3 years)
  • Degree levels: Associate's, Bachelor's, Master's, and Doctorate
  • Limitations: 2024 data is preliminary; doesn't capture bootcamps or non-accredited programs

Key Insights:
  • The doctoral paradox: Women's representation is often higher at Master's than Bachelor's, but drops again at Doctorate — suggesting barriers to academic careers
  • Field variation matters: Cybersecurity and Information Science tend to have higher women's representation than core Computer Science or Computer Engineering
  • Historical context: In 1984, women earned 37% of CS Bachelor's degrees — today's 24% represents a regression, not progress from a low baseline
  • At this rate: Reaching gender parity (50%) would take over 65 years

Act 2 The Age Cliff

Women who do enter tech often don't stay. Bureau of Labor Statistics data reveals a sharp drop in women's representation around age 35 — the "cliff" where tech careers derail. This pattern persists across occupations and has barely improved over the past decade.

Data Source: BLS Current Population Survey (CPS)
  • What it is: The primary source of U.S. labor force statistics, surveying approximately 60,000 households monthly
  • What we used: Annual averages from Table 11 (employed by occupation and sex) and Table 11b (employed by occupation and age)
  • Years covered: 2015-2024 (10 consecutive years)
  • Occupations tracked: Software Developers, Computer Systems Analysts, Database Admins, InfoSec Analysts, Programmers, Network Admins
  • Important caveat: BLS doesn't publish age x sex x occupation directly. Women counts by age are estimated by multiplying (total in age bracket) x (% women in occupation)

Key Insights:
  • The 35-44 cliff is real: Across nearly every tech occupation, women's numbers peak in the 25-34 bracket then drop sharply — often by 20-30%
  • It's not just motherhood: While family responsibilities play a role, research shows hostile work environments, lack of advancement, and "bro culture" drive women out
  • Continued decline: The drop continues through 45-54 and 55-64, meaning women who leave rarely return to technical roles
  • Near-stagnant progress: Software Developers went from 17.9% women (2015) to 20.3% (2024) — just 2.4 percentage points in 9 years
  • Lost experience: The cliff means companies lose senior women just as they'd be moving into leadership

Act 3 The Intersectional View

The cliff doesn't affect all women equally. When we examine the intersection of race and age, distinct patterns emerge. Some groups face steeper cliffs than others, and the COVID-19 pandemic may have affected different communities differently.

Data Source: NSCG (National Survey of College Graduates)
  • What it is: A biennial survey conducted by NSF through the National Center for Science and Engineering Statistics (NCSES)
  • What we used: Public-use microdata files filtered to "Computer and mathematical scientists" (N2OCPRMG=1)
  • Sample size: ~130,000 respondents per wave, representing U.S. college graduates
  • Years covered: 2019 (pre-COVID baseline) and 2023 (post-COVID)
  • Variables used: SEX, RACETHM (race/ethnicity), AGEGR (age group), WTSURVY (survey weight)
  • Important caveat: Small subgroups (n < 20) are suppressed per NCSES guidelines to protect privacy

Act 4 The Data Cliff

Just as we began to measure progress, the data itself is disappearing. Starting in 2024-2025, major tech companies have stopped publishing annual diversity reports. Without this data, we lose the ability to hold companies accountable or track whether initiatives are working.

Data Source: Big Tech Annual Diversity Reports
  • What it is: Self-reported workforce composition data that major tech companies began voluntarily publishing around 2014, following pressure from activists and journalists
  • Companies tracked: Google, Apple, Microsoft, Meta (Facebook), and Amazon
  • Metrics reported: Women as % of overall workforce, and women as % of technical roles
  • Years covered: 2014-2024 (varies by company; Google has the longest history)
  • Important caveats: Data is self-reported and not independently audited; companies define "technical roles" differently; Amazon only reports overall workforce, not technical breakdown; reports often exclude contractors

Key Insights:
  • Progress was real but slow: Google went from 17% to 27% women in technical roles over 10 years — meaningful progress, but still far from parity
  • The reporting cliff is political: Companies stopped publishing DEI reports following the 2024 political shift and backlash against diversity initiatives
  • What we lose: Without annual reports, researchers, journalists, and advocates cannot track whether companies are making progress or backsliding
  • Apple and Amazon remain: As of 2025, only Apple and Amazon have committed to continuing diversity reporting — and Amazon doesn't break out technical roles
  • The irony: Just as the data showed that DEI efforts were slowly working, the companies stopped measuring
  • Government data isn't enough: BLS and Census data have 1-2 year lags and can't identify individual companies
